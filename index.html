Addison demonstrates exceptional land use diversity — a balanced mix of commercial, residential, and civic uses that sets it apart from its neighboring city of Dallas.

Land use diversity is a key indicator of urban vitality. Cities with varied, mixed-use environments tend to generate more economic activity, support walkability, and reduce infrastructure inefficiencies. This report examines Addison's 2025 land use composition and benchmarks it against Dallas, using parcel-level data from the Land Use Diversity dashboard.

Addison has nearly twice as many significant land use categories as Dallas, signaling a genuinely mixed urban environment.

Dallas's land use is more concentrated, with residential alone consuming over a third of total land area.

Office space leads in Addison, reflecting its role as a dense suburban employment hub in the Dallas–Fort Worth metro.

Key Findings

Addison: A Balanced Urban Mix

Addison's land use profile is notably even across multiple categories. No single use dominates overwhelmingly. Office (18.1%), Residential (15.5%), and Retail (13.5%) each hold significant shares, followed by Hospitality (10.2%) and Multifamily (8.9%). This reflects intentional planning around Addison's restaurant row, corporate campuses, and walkable town core.

  • Office 18.1%
  • Residential 15.5%
  • Retail 13.5%
  • Hospitality 10.2%
  • Multifamily 8.9%
  • Industrial 5.5%

Dallas: Residential Dominance

In contrast, Dallas is heavily weighted toward residential land use at 35.1%, with a substantial 16.5% share classified as undeveloped land. Industrial (13.6%) and a secondary residential tier round out the top uses. While Dallas's sheer scale demands more residential land, the large undeveloped share suggests ongoing growth pressure and lower land use efficiency compared to Addison.

  • Residential 35.1%
  • Land (vacant) 16.5%
  • Industrial 13.6%
  • Park / Public 3.2%
  • Retail 2.7%
